Author: Bharath Arun Gandhimani  |  Date: 12/05/2026
Description: Fully author-driven interactive narrative visualisation communicating findings about educational infrastructure investment, socioeconomic equity, and household travel behaviour in Victoria. Targeted at policymakers and transport planners. Reader progresses through a fixed 7-step sequence — one analytical layer per step.
Design Rationale
The stepper enforces a deliberate reading order — investment geography is established first before socioeconomic or behavioural patterns are introduced. This prevents a policymaker from jumping to conclusions without the geographic context that frames the entire argument. Chart types (choropleth, slope chart, lollipop) are drawn from Sheet 1 Ideas #7, #6, and the geographic/map category respectively. No filters or dropdowns — narrative control stays with the author. Chart family (choropleth, lollipop, slope, grouped bar, scatter) is entirely distinct from Design B (scatter, violin, small multiples) and Design C (dot strip, bump, connected dot) — these three designs use non-overlapping visual encodings, satisfying the FDS requirement for genuine chart-level distinctness across Sheets 2–4.

Operations
[B] Click "Next →"
⇒
Content in [D] and [E] fades out. Next step's chart and narrative text fade in. Step indicator [A] advances — current circle fills solid grey (visited), next circle activates black. On the final step the Next button is replaced by a "Restart" option.
[C] Click "← Previous"
⇒
Returns to the prior step. [A] updates to reflect the previous active step. [C] is hidden when the reader is on Step 1 — there is no "before" the opening frame.
[A] Click a visited step circle
⇒
Jumps directly to that completed step for review. Unvisited future circles remain greyed out and unclickable — the reader cannot skip ahead to content they have not yet reached.
[D] Hover on chart element
⇒
A tooltip appears showing exact values — percentage, count, LGA name. This is the only interactivity within a step. No filters, no dropdowns, no view changes are available.
[A] Step circle — state logic
⇒
Visited circles show filled grey. Active circle shows filled black. Unvisited future circles show white with light grey border and are unclickable. State maintained for the full session.
Step 7 — Final step reached
⇒
The 29-LGA gap callout and policy implication text are revealed. Data source links and a "Restart" button appear. The [B] Next button is hidden. Reader has completed the full narrative arc.
Focus / Parti — Step Transition Mechanism
The closing step only makes sense once all prior evidence has been absorbed — this is what separates Design A from B and C. Chart types per step: Step 2 → Choropleth (geographic category, Sheet 1); Step 3 → Lollipop (Sheet 1 Idea #6); Step 4 → Slope Chart (Sheet 1 Idea #7).

Discussion
Pros
  • Sees evidence in exact order as intended — no confusion about where to start.
  • One chart, one explanation per step keeps things simple and focused for a non-technical policymaker audience.
  • Opens with geographic framing (choropleth). Closes with what remains unknown (29-LGA gap). Clean narrative arc.
  • Works well with non-technical audiences like policymakers who need framing before data.
  • Easy to implement in R Shiny using a reactive step counter with conditional panel rendering.
  • Chart types in this design — choropleth, lollipop, slope, grouped bar, scatter — address geographic and categorical comparisons. This is a different perceptual task family from Design B (distributional / bivariate) and Design C (strip / ranking / paired), ensuring genuine design-space breadth across Sheets 2–4 as required by the FDS methodology.
  • Hover tooltips as the sole interactivity keeps implementation scope controlled and avoids cognitive overload.
Cons
  • Cannot compare two charts side by side — reader must go back and forth.
